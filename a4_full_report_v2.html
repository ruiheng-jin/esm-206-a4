DATA AND METHODS

The data used in this report was collected by researchers from the Santa Barbara Coastal Long Term Ecological Research program (SBC LTER). The data includes observations of California spiny lobsters at five locations, two of which are Marine Protected Area (MPA). Three variables are studied in this report: year of observation, site of observation, size of lobster (mm). Two-sample, two-sided, unpaired tests ( = 0.05) and cohens d are performed for comparisons of mean lobster sizes between MPA sites and non-MPA sites, and between 2012 and 2018. Data analysis is performed using RStudio (Version 1.2.1335).

RESULTS

RESULTS A

CHANGES IN ANNUAL LOBSTER ABUNDANCE AT FIVE SITES

Isla Vista has the most noticable increase in lobster abundance from 2012 to 2018. Additionally, further analysis is needed to explain the sudden spike in lobster abundance in Isla Vista after 2016. Naples and Mohawk have similar increasing trends, although Naples experienced an increase between 2017 and 2018, while Mohawk a decrease. Carpinteria experienced a large increase between 2016 and 2017, but also a large decrease the following year. The The Arroyo Quemados abundance remained low throughout the 2012-2018 period (Figure 2).

RESULTS B

SHIFTS IN LOBSTER SIZE DISTRIBUTION FROM 2012 TO 2018

There are two marine protected area (MPA), Naples and Isla Vista. Naples has a higher percentage of larger lobsters in 2018 than in 2012. Ilsa Vista, another MPA site, has an even bigger shift is lobster size. There are a lot of smaller lobsters in 2012, but they become much bigger in 2018. The change in size distributions for Naples and Ilsa Vista makes sense because these sites are marine protected areas, these lobsters have been allowed to grow old and larger in size. For the other three sites, there is a shift towards larger lobster over time, but the shift is much smaller. The biggest shift in lobster size over time for the non mpa sites is seen at the site Carpinteria (Figure 3).

1) Mean lobster size at MPA sites in 2012 and 2018

  • Mean lobster sizes differ significantly at MPA sites between 2012 and 2018 (t(32.5) = -4.69, p = 4.810^{-5}).
  • The 95% confidence interval is (-14.62, -5.77).
  • The effect size is large (Cohens d = -0.87).
  • Mean lobster sizes at MPA sites in 2012 and 2018 are 67.38, 77.57 mm, respectively. The difference in means is -10.19 mm.

2) Mean lobster size at non-MPA sites in 2012 and 2018

  • Mean lobster sizes at non-MPA sites do not differ significantly between 2012 and 2018 (t(296.01) = 1.33, p = 0.1831).
  • The 95% confidence interval is (-0.62, 3.23).
  • The effect size is negligible (Cohens d = 0.121).
  • Mean lobster sizes at non-MPA sites in 2012 and 2018 are 74.92, 73.62 mm, respectively. The difference in means is 1.3 mm.

3) Mean lobster size at MPA and non-MPA sites in 2012

  • Mean lobster sizes differ significantly between MPA and non-MPA sites in 2012 (t(42.09) = -3.25, p = 0.0023).
  • The 95% confidence interval is (-12.23, -2.86).
  • The effect size is medium (Cohens d = -0.61).
  • In 2012, mean lobster sizes at MPA and non-MPA sites are 67.38, 74.92 mm, respectively. The difference in means is -7.55 mm.

4) Mean lobster size at MPA and non-MPA sites in 2018

  • Mean lobster sizes differ significantly between MPA and non-MPA sites in 2012 (t(1239.51) = 7.31, p = 4.910^{-13}).
  • The 95% confidence interval is (2.89, 5.01).
  • The effect size is small (Cohens d = 0.351).
  • In 2018, mean lobster sizes at MPA and non-MPA sites are 77.57, 73.62 mm, respectively. The difference in means is 3.95 mm.
